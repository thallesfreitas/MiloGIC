If you've built a brilliant solution, this Challenge is for you

You’ll need more than a brilliant idea to compete in the Challenge. You’ll need a viable business solution that’s already in place and generating revenue. (Even if only $1.) All entries will need to answer the following:

What is it?

When completing the entry form, you’ll describe your solution in its entirety. Everything from tactical details and team members, to value propositions and revenue models. This will highlight your innovative thinking.

What wants is?

Market opportunity is crucial to any endeavor. You’ll tell us not only who’s currently implementing your idea, but also which other clients might be interested. This will display the scalability of your solution.

What’s it worth?

Money is a measure everyone understands. That’s why you’ll break down existing and potential sales, plus demand triggers. You’ll also detail what’s needed in terms of time and materials support. All will display the potential for your solution’s ongoing profitability.

Where does it fit?

In some ways, innovation must work inside the box. You’ll explain how your idea fits in with lines of service, business imperatives, and go-to-market strategies. This will illustrate how your solution advances goals at the enterprise level.

Rules are meant to be broken. Except these.

As an innovator, you’re probably comfortable bending – if not breaking – more than a few rules. We applaud that spirit. That’s why there aren’t a lot of restrictive regulations and parameters attached to the Global Innovation Challenge. There are only two caveats. But we do take them seriously.

1

Your solution has to already be in place and making money for a PwC client. The Global Innovation Challenge isn’t a conceptual forum. It’s a showcase for real, revenue-generating business solutions that solve real business problems.

2

You need to complete the entry form in its entirety. Every section. It’s our way of properly vetting the hundreds of submissions we receive. You can have the best solution the firm has ever seen. But an incomplete entry form immediately disqualifies you. Why would you want to miss out on a technicality?
